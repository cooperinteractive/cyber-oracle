Quantum Tunneling

How particles walk through walls
Act 01
In the classical world, walls work
START GOAL
Barrier
⟵ Bounced
A classical particle - think of a tennis ball - needs enough energy to climb over a wall. If the wall is taller than the ball's energy, it bounces off. Every time. No exceptions. This is the world we live in: doors stop people, walls stop balls, firewalls stop packets.
Act 02
In the quantum world, walls leak
|ψ⟩ wave tunnel zone most reflects some tunnels
A quantum particle isn't a ball - it's a wave of probability (the squiggle marked |ψ⟩). When that wave hits a wall it can't classically cross, most of it bounces back… but a small piece leaks straight through to the other side. The particle didn't climb over. It didn't break the wall. It just had a non-zero chance of being there. And in quantum mechanics, "non-zero chance" means it happens.
Why it works
Particles don't have exact positions - only probabilities. The probability tail extends through the barrier, so the particle can be found on the other side.
Where you've seen it
Tunneling powers the sun (fusion), modern flash memory, and the scanning tunneling microscopes that image individual atoms.

A classical attacker has to climb over your defenses - find a misconfiguration, brute-force a password, exploit a known bug. A quantum-equipped attacker is more like the wave: they don't go around your firewall, they compute through the math that makes it work. Shor's algorithm on a sufficiently large quantum computer would tunnel straight through RSA and ECC encryption - the cryptographic walls protecting most of the internet today.
Real threat: HNDL
Harvest Now, Decrypt Later: nation-states are recording your encrypted traffic today, planning to tunnel through it later when quantum hardware catches up.
The defense
Post-quantum cryptography (Kyber, Dilithium, lattice-based schemes) - math problems that even quantum computers can't tunnel through.

The big idea

Quantum tunneling is the universe's reminder that "impossible" depends on what kind of physics you're playing with. A wall that stops every ball can leak waves. A cryptosystem that's safe against every classical computer can fall to a quantum one. The work is to build defenses based on problems that nothing we know of - quantum or classical - can tunnel through.
