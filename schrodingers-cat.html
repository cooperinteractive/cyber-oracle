Schrodinger's Cat

The most famous thought experiment in physics
Act 01

The setup

In 1935, physicist Erwin Schrodinger proposed a thought experiment to show how strange quantum mechanics gets when you scale it up. Put a cat in a sealed box. Inside the box: a tiny radioactive sample, a Geiger counter, and a vial of poison. If the radioactive atom decays, the counter triggers, the vial breaks, and the cat dies. If it does not decay, the cat lives. Schrodinger was not actually proposing this as an experiment. He was making a point.

Why a cat
Schrodinger picked something macroscopic and unmistakably alive or dead to make the absurdity vivid. A radioactive atom in superposition is fine. A cat in superposition is bizarre.
No real cats
No actual animals were ever harmed for this. It is a thought experiment, not a lab procedure.
Act 02

Until you look, the cat is both

🙀

Box sealed. Cat in superposition.

While the box is sealed, the radioactive atom is in superposition. It has not decayed AND it has decayed, simultaneously. By the rules of quantum mechanics, that means the cat is also in superposition. Both alive and dead. Not "we don't know which one." Genuinely both, with full mathematical reality. The moment you open the box, the wavefunction collapses and you find one outcome. The act of looking changes reality.

The deeper question
What counts as "looking"? A human? A camera? A particle? This is called the measurement problem, and physicists still argue about it 90 years later.
Many-worlds
Some physicists say there is no collapse. Reality just splits. In one branch the cat lives. In another, it does not. Both are equally real. You only experience one.

Kyber
Stays sealed

Every encrypted message you send today is like a sealed box. Right now, no one can open it. But somewhere, attackers are storing those boxes, waiting. When a sufficiently powerful quantum computer arrives, it will open every old box at once, collapsing years of harvested traffic into readable plaintext. This is called Harvest Now, Decrypt Later. The question is not whether the cat is alive. The question is whether your data is still safe when someone finally opens the lid.

What stays sealed
Post-quantum algorithms like CRYSTALS-Kyber and CRYSTALS-Dilithium, standardized by NIST in 2024, are designed to stay sealed even against quantum computers.
Migration urgency
Anything you encrypted today that needs to stay secret for 10+ years should already be using post-quantum cryptography. The boxes you seal now will be opened by tomorrow's quantum computers.

The big idea

Schrodinger's cat is really a parable about observation, uncertainty, and the limits of common sense at quantum scale. For security, it is also a warning: the secrets you seal today are not safe forever. The longer the box stays closed, the better the tools to crack it. Choose your locks accordingly.
